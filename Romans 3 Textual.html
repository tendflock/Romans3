§ A note on Psalm 53 Psalm 14 has a twin. Which one is Paul quoting? Expand Collapse

Psalm 14 and Psalm 53 are Hebrew twins — almost the same psalm, copied twice into the Psalter. Psalm 53 sits in what scholars call the Elohistic Psalter and swaps the divine name YHWH for the more generic Elohim, with a reworked judgment line about God scattering the bones of the besieger. They look nearly identical in the verses Paul quotes — so which one is he on? The answer turns out to be hiding in a single word: χρηστότητα.

What differs
  • YHWH is used throughout the surrounding psalms.
  • Verse 5 reads: "they are in great fear where no fear was."
  • The LXX verse 3 was later expanded to include Paul's full catena.
  • Elohim replaces YHWH — a trait of the Elohistic Psalter.
  • Verse 5 rewrites the judgment: God scatters the bones of the besieger.
  • The LXX of Psalm 52 was never expanded with Paul's catena.

Verdict: Paul is on Psalm 14 (LXX 13). χρηστότητα is the LXX 13 reading; the LXX 52 doublet has ἀγαθόν instead. And the secondary expansion in Psalm 13 LXX — the one that absorbs Paul's catena back into the psalm — appears only in the Psalm 14 branch, never in its twin.

and another — this one decides which psalm
χρηστότητα kindness Psalm 14 or Psalm 53?

Psalm 14 and Psalm 53 are Hebrew doublets — almost the same psalm, twice in the Psalter. In Greek they're numbered Psalm 13 and Psalm 52, and at one word they split: Paul's word matches Psalm 13, not Psalm 52.

Romans 3:12 · Paul's Greek
οὐκ ἔστιν ποιῶν χρηστότητα
There is no one doing kindness.
LXX · Psalm 13:3 (= MT Psalm 14)
οὐκ ἔστι ποιῶν χρηστότητα
No one doing kindness. ↑ matches Paul
LXX · Psalm 52:3 (= MT Psalm 53)
οὐκ ἔστι ποιῶν ἀγαθόν
No one doing good. ≠ different word in the doublet

One word isn't proof. But across the catena, Paul's wording tracks the Greek often enough that a Greek text form is the simplest explanation.

Later expansion ↓

Every line that lands in the expanded Psalm is a phrase from Paul — Psalms 5, 140, 10, Isaiah 59, Psalm 36. Critical editors judge this expansion secondary. The simplest explanation: later scribes, familiar with Romans, wrote Paul's catena back into the margins of the Psalter, and from there it entered the text.
